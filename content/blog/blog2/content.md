After my bachelor's degree, I went straight into a job.

I spent years building products as a software engineer — writing code, shipping features, fixing bugs, moving fast. A lot of my work was about *doing*: implementing ideas, solving problems, delivering outcomes. Listening existed, of course, but mostly as a means to an end — gathering requirements, clarifying tasks, aligning on next steps.

It worked. Things got built. Products shipped.

But it wasn't until I started my PhD that I realized how much I had underestimated the act of truly listening.

### From Doing to Paying Attention

Starting a PhD didn't just introduce me to new methods or research questions. It quietly changed how I paid attention to people.

I found myself sitting with participants, asking open-ended questions, and realizing that the most important moments weren't always in the answers themselves — but in the pauses, the hesitations, the stories that surfaced when I stopped rushing to the next question on my script.

Someone would say something vague. Earlier, I might have moved on. Now, I found myself asking:

> *"Could you tell me more about that?"*  
> *"What did that experience look like for you?"*  
> *"What happened next?"*

And suddenly, the conversation would open up.

Listening, I learned, is not passive. It's not just waiting for your turn to speak. It's active, deliberate, and often slower than we're used to.

### Listening Beyond What Your Job May Be

Over time, I began to see listening as something much bigger than a research skill.

Whether you are listening to answer a research question, trying to bring an idea to life, or talking to a client in your day-to-day work, the core act is the same. You are trying to understand someone else's story and experience — on their terms, not yours.

What makes the difference isn't having the perfect question. It's how you follow through.

When someone says, "That was frustrating," you can either move on — or pause and ask:

> *"When you say frustrating, what do you mean by that?"*  
> *"What part of that experience stood out most to you?"*

Those small moments of curiosity are often where the real story lives.

Without listening, we miss the very thing that gives meaning to what we build. Stories and lived experiences often become the foundation of trust, clarity, and even the value of what we create.

### Why Stories Matter More Than Numbers Alone

We can run surveys. We can analyze metrics. We can look at trends and dashboards.

Those things matter. They give us direction.

But qualitative data goes beyond that. Individual stories reveal context — the *why* behind decisions, the workarounds people adopt, the frustrations they don't always label as problems.

Sometimes the most useful insight comes after a simple follow-up:

> *"Is that usually the case, or was this a one-off?"*  
> *"How does that compare to other experiences you've had?"*

Experiences don't always show up cleanly in numbers, but they shape behavior in powerful ways.

You don't need to empathize all the time. But you do need to pay attention.

### The Discipline of Not Steering the Conversation

One of the hardest lessons I've learned is resisting the urge to lead people toward the answers I want.

It's tempting to ask questions that reassure us — that our idea is worth building, that our hypothesis makes sense, that our research question is valid. But real listening requires letting go of that need for confirmation.

Sometimes that means acknowledging what someone shared and then stepping back:

> *"That's really interesting — thank you for sharing that."*  
> *"Building on what you just said, I'd like to ask…"*

And sometimes it means allowing silence to do some of the work.

When we stop expecting a particular answer, we often hear much more.

### What Careful Listening Makes Possible

Careful listening builds rapport, but more importantly, it creates space. Space for people to explain themselves fully. Space for contrast.

After talking about what worked, you can gently shift and ask:

> *"We've talked about positive experiences — what about the challenges?"*  

These transitions matter. They signal respect. They tell people their story doesn't need to be neat or polished to be worth hearing.

Over time, I've learned that the quality of a conversation often depends less on how clever the questions are and more on how willing I am to truly listen to the answers.

### I'm Still Learning

Listening is not something I've mastered. It's a practice I'm still learning — across research, work, and everyday conversations. But it has changed how I see people's stories; not as inputs to validate my ideas, but as experiences that deserve attention in their own right.
